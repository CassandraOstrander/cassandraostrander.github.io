A Look at Amazon’s AI Hiring Tool and the Ethics Behind It

Over the last year or so, I’ve spent a lot more time (then I’d like to admit) updating my resume, writing cover letters, and applying for jobs. Like many other job seekers, I’ve found myself wondering if a real person ever looked at my application or if it was filtered out before anyone had the chance. As companies continue to use artificial intelligence (AI) to help sort through hundreds or even thousands of applications, that question becomes more relevant than ever. AI has become a valuable tool for employers because it can save time by quickly scanning resumes and identifying candidates who appear to be the best fit. However, when AI starts making decisions that affect people’s careers, it’s important to ask whether those decisions are being made fairly. Can an algorithm be unbiased, or does it simply learn from the people who created it and the data it was given?

One of the most well-known examples of this issue is Amazon’s experimental AI hiring tool. Although it was designed to make recruiting more efficient, the system ended up creating ethical concerns about fairness, bias, and accountability. In this blog, I’ll take a closer look at what happened, why it happened, and what it teaches us about using AI in hiring.

So… Where Did Things Go Wrong?

This isn’t just a story about Amazon. It’s a reminder that data analysts play an important role in how AI systems are built. The data used to train an AI model can directly influence the decisions it makes, which is why ethics should be considered from the very beginning of the development process.

So back in 2014, Amazon secretly started developing an AI recruiting tool. It was designed to look at 10 years of hiring data and rate incoming resumes on a scale of one to five based on patterns from previous hiring decisions. The problem was that, for most of those 10 years, the tech industry was heavily male-dominated, so most of the people being hired were men. Over time, the AI learned that pattern and developed a gender bias. It even started discarding resumes that included words like “women’s,” such as “women’s chess club.” Once Amazon realized what was happening, they scrapped the project in 2018. Since then, the company has taken a more careful approach by using newer AI tools that go through much more testing before being used to help reduce bias in the hiring process.

When History Repeats Itself…

One of the biggest ethical concerns with Amazon’s hiring tool was fairness. AI is supposed to help make decisions based on skills and qualifications, not someone’s gender. Unfortunately, because the system was trained using hiring data from a male-dominated industry, it started associating male applicants with being more successful. As a result, qualified women were being ranked lower simply because of patterns the AI had learned from the past.

Behind the Curtain

Ooohh transparency, what a funny thought when it comes to a company secretly screening your resume without your knowledge. Which brings me to my other ethical concern. Most people applying for a job probably assume a recruiter or hiring manager is looking at their resume. In Amazon’s case, many applicants had no idea an AI system was helping evaluate them, and they certainly didn’t know how it was making those decisions. If someone was rejected because the AI ranked their resume lower, there was no explanation or opportunity to understand why. Without that transparency, it’s hard for people to trust the hiring process.

Transparency asks questions like:

Did applicants know AI was screening them?

Did they know how the AI ranked resumes?

If they were rejected, could they find out why?

Could they challenge the AI’s decision?

The answer to most of those questions was no. Transparency doesn’t mean a company has to reveal every detail of its AI model. It does mean applicants should understand when AI is being used and have some idea of how important decisions about their applications are being made.

Own Your Mistakes

Another ethical issue I noticed was accountability. When Amazon discovered its AI hiring tool was unfairly ranking candidates, the company shut the project down. While that was an important step, I couldn’t find a public apology to the applicants who may have been affected or much explanation about what went wrong. It made me wonder who should be held responsible when AI makes decisions that impact people’s lives.

For what it’s worth, in my opinion; responsibility shouldn’t fall on the AI itself. AI can only work with the data and instructions given. It’s up to the company and the people developing the system to make sure it’s tested properly, monitored for bias, and corrected when problems are found. That’s what accountability looks like in data science.

Will AI Take Our Jobs?

So in all fairness, mistakes were made and corrected by Amazon. But this just teaches us how important it is for AI systems to be monitored carefully for correction; AI is a tool and not a final decision maker.

Think about it like this:

AI can process 100,000 resumes much faster than a person.

AI can spot patterns humans might miss.

AI can save companies a huge amount of time.

Those are all positives.

But…

AI doesn’t understand fairness.

AI doesn’t understand the context.

AI doesn’t stop and think; it simply follows the patterns it learned.

And THAT is where humans come in.

Final Thoughts

The biggest lesson I took away from this case is that AI shouldn’t be making important decisions on its own. It can be an incredible tool for sorting through large amounts of data, but there should always be people checking the results. If Amazon had been monitoring the system more closely, they may have caught the gender bias much earlier. AI can help speed up the hiring process, but it shouldn’t replace human judgment when someone’s career is on the line.
